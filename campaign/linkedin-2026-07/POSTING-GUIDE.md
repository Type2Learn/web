# LinkedIn posting guide

Use the posts as standalone pieces rather than uploading the entire sequence at once. A practical rhythm is two or three posts per week, followed by the featured overview once the audience has seen the core principles.

Append this same invitation to each LinkedIn caption:

> Are you neurodivergent and willing to help us challenge assumptions and shape Type2Learn? Email **contact@type2learn.tech**. Sharing is voluntary; no diagnosis details are needed. If you are under 18, ask a parent or guardian to contact us.

Suggested core hashtags: `#Type2Learn #Neurodiversity #AccessibleLearning #EdTech`

Every final image includes a purpose-made pose of the owner-supplied white-and-blue Type2Learn bunny. The bunny appears as the active speaker in the help-us-shape-Type2Learn invitation.

## 01 — The promise

Caption opener: Learning should require action, not just attention. Type2Learn is being built around a simple route: encounter an idea, recall it, produce a response, correct it, use it, and return to it. What helps an idea stay with you?

Alt text: Type2Learn poster with a learner working at a laptop and notebook. Headline: “Learn by typing. Build knowledge that stays.” It explains the active learning route and asks what helps an idea stay. The neuroinclusive use case is learners needing active practice, controllable supports, and a route back to the idea.

## 02 — Active, not passive

Caption opener: Watching can support learning. It is not the whole learning process. We are designing Type2Learn to add a clear objective, an active response, useful feedback, application, and return. Where does your learning become active?

Alt text: Type2Learn poster showing an educator and learner working together. Headline: “Pressing play is not the same as practising.” The neuroinclusive use case is learners who benefit from one bounded action instead of passive, unstructured content.

## 03 — Beyond speed

Caption opener: A fast response is not automatically an understood response. Type2Learn is not intended to be a public typing-speed race. What should meaningful progress measure?

Alt text: Type2Learn poster with a learner typing in a library. Headline: “Fast isn’t the same as learned.” It defines progress as meaningful response, correction, and learning evidence. The neuroinclusive use case is learners whose understanding should never be reduced to typing speed.

## 04 — Dignity and control

Caption opener: Motion, sound, spacing, pacing, literal instructions, and response options can change whether a person can participate. Different controls should never mean lower expectations of dignity. What control would have helped you?

Alt text: Type2Learn poster with a young adult learner at a laptop. Headline: “Different minds need different controls—not different expectations of dignity.” The neuroinclusive use case is learners needing motion, sound, spacing, pacing, or instruction controls without lowered expectations.

## 05 — Support without a label

Caption opener: A learner should not have to disclose a diagnosis before choosing a useful control. Settings are participation tools, not clinical conclusions. What support should everyone be able to choose?

Alt text: Type2Learn poster showing a learner using cards beside a computer. Headline: “Support should not require a label.” The neuroinclusive use case is anyone needing support without disclosing a diagnosis or accepting a label.

## 06 — One clear next action

Caption opener: Starting is easier when the objective is visible, the current action is bounded, and pausing will not erase the work. What makes starting easier for you?

Alt text: Type2Learn poster with a learner working in a quiet study. Headline: “One clear next action.” The neuroinclusive use case is learners facing initiation or working-memory barriers who need one visible next step.

## 07 — Dignified correction

Caption opener: Correction can strengthen an idea without diminishing the learner. Compare, explain the mismatch, rebuild, and try again. What did useful feedback feel like?

Alt text: Type2Learn poster showing an educator and older teen calmly reviewing work. Headline: “Correction should make the idea stronger—not the learner smaller.” The neuroinclusive use case is learners needing specific, non-shaming feedback and a real chance to reconstruct the answer.

## 08 — Calm return

Caption opener: Pausing should not destroy progress. A response, cursor, hints, settings, and next step should still be there when a learner returns. What would make returning feel possible?

Alt text: Type2Learn poster showing a university-age woman returning to a waiting laptop and notebook. Headline: “Paused doesn’t mean lost.” The neuroinclusive use case is learners whose attention, energy, access, or routine changes and who need progress to survive the pause.

## 09 — Word Builder

Caption opener: Word Builder is an adapted concept in development: hear the pattern, spell it, reconstruct it, connect meaning, use it, and return later. Typing is the response channel, not the learning goal. What makes a new word stay with you?

Alt text: Type2Learn module poster with the Word Builder identity. Headline: “Words are built—not flashed past.” The neuroinclusive use case is learners needing structured literacy, reconstruction, meaning, and delayed return, including many dyslexic learners.

## 10 — Focus Sprint

Caption opener: Focus Sprint is an adapted concept in development: a visible Now → Next → Done route, one current action, optional short breaks, saved work, and a calm return. What helps you re-enter the work?

Alt text: Type2Learn module poster with the Focus Sprint identity. Headline: “Focus needs a path—not a score.” The neuroinclusive use case is ADHD and other neurodivergent learners choosing visible steps, flexible pacing, and calm re-entry.

## 11 — Predictable Path

Caption opener: Predictable Path is an adapted concept in development: preview the objective, steps, interaction, duration range, sensory events, and what changes next. What do you wish you knew before a lesson began?

Alt text: Type2Learn module poster with the Predictable Path identity. Headline: “Predictability should inform—not restrict.” The neuroinclusive use case is autistic and other learners choosing predictable transitions, literal instructions, and sensory control.

## 12 — Evidence, not surveillance

Caption opener: Learning evidence should show the work, correction, support context, and current status. It should not infer diagnosis or reduce a person to one score. What should educators see—and what should remain private?

Alt text: Type2Learn poster showing a small group learning with an educator. Headline: “Evidence, not surveillance.” The neuroinclusive use case is learners deserving useful learning evidence without surveillance or inferred labels.

## 13 — Private by default

Caption opener: Privacy is not a policy added at the end. It is a product decision: no learner-data sale, no targeted advertising, no diagnosis inferred from typing, and no default public sharing for minors. What would earn your trust?

Alt text: Type2Learn poster showing a family looking at a laptop. Headline: “Private by default is a product decision.” The neuroinclusive use case is learners whose accessibility preferences may be sensitive and must remain private by default.

## 14 — Made with, not for

Caption opener: Lived experience should change barriers, wording, controls, sensory load, prototypes, and release decisions—not decorate a launch story. What are we missing?

Alt text: Type2Learn poster showing a collaborative design discussion. Headline: “Lived experience should change the product.” The neuroinclusive use case is neurodivergent people whose lived experience should change actual product decisions.

Caption opener: The Type2Learn website recorded 1,372 unique visitors in the previous seven days, with requests from multiple regions. Reach is useful; listening is the next step. Where are you joining from?
